In B2B, the first value from AI doesn't come from agentic architecture — it comes from the most banal prompt, "find me companies that could buy my product," *provided* you've described the customer profile properly first. And it's the fast result that convinces a skeptical stakeholder, not a beautiful deck about the future. You need one simple case where the need and the money are already burning, and a trivial prompt that puts the fire out.

## The setup

- Client — a distributor of chemical raw materials, pigments, and equipment (e.g. spectrophotometers).
- Task — sell off >10 tonnes of dead stock (epoxy resin, hardener, plasticizer) with expiry dates approaching.
- Deadline — 3 months.
- Price — around 2M ₽; willing to drop to cost or slightly below (purchased at a high exchange rate).

## What I did

- Opened a ChatGPT chat.
- Generated application areas for epoxy resins and components (it's a commodity — plenty of data online).
- Refined the customer profile: a buyer ready to take the whole volume; production in the Leningrad region or nearby, so shipping wouldn't eat the price advantage.
- Researched companies against those criteria with a simple prompt.

## The result

Out came roughly **100 companies**. We handed them to a junior sales rep for cold outreach (emails and calls). Among them was a company that bought **2 of the 3 components**.

**Second move:** I asked ChatGPT "what other channels could we use?" It gave the obvious advice — Avito, price aggregators. We listed on Avito, and within a couple of weeks the **third component** — the one that hadn't sold — was gone.

After that the client agreed to go further and try AI on larger chunks of the process. Because the old approaches hadn't produced this in 3 months, and this did — fast and cheap.
